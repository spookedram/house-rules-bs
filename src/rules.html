Getting Started

Before you can start playing House Rules RPG, you'll need:

  • an Architect
  • a Quest
  • 1 or more Players
  • with at least 1 character each
  • An understanding of the rules
  • a dice set or an RNG

Taking Actions

Free Actions

During the game, Players will want their character to interact with the world and will do so by taking an action. Actions with little difficulty, such as speaking bluntly or opening an unlocked door, are free actions. If the action that the Player wants their character to take is more difficult then they'll need to roll a difficulty check.

Difficulty Checks

Throwing a knife to cut a swinging rope, disarming a ticking time bomb, and summoning an unknowable beast from another dimension are actions much more difficult than opening an unlocked door. Actions with any level of difficulty require a difficulty check. The Player will describe the action they want to attempt and the Architect decides what modifier is being checked and how difficult the action being taken is (as a number which they may or may not share with the player). Then the Player rolls a d20 to see if they succeed or fail the action. For example:

Player: "I want to use my new lockpick on the cage."
Architect: "Alright, roll a dexterity check."
Architect (thinking): Probably a DC of 14...
Player: "Nice! I rolled a 16!"
Architect: "You pick the lock. Sadly, you've also woken the circus bear inside..."

You might have noticed that the Player's action had an ability that was being checked. When an action is likely going to result from a test of a character's abilities, the difficulty check is modified by the character's score for the ability being checked.

Ability Scores

Every character and creature in House Rules RPG has 6 ability scores, represented by a number between 1 and 20. The abilities cover a wide variety of character attributes and actions that can be taken. These 6 abilities are:

Strength Represents muscle and physical power
Dexterity Represents agility, reflexes, and balance
Constitution Represents health, stamina, and endurance
Intelligence Represents critical thinking, learning, and logic
Wisdom Represents willpower, common sense, and intuition
Charisma Represents personality, likability, and ability to lead

Characters start with 10 points on each score, because 10 points means 0 modifier. Each number greater or less than 10 adds a modifier to ability checks. For example:

Player: "Does Killgrog understand the hieroglyphs?"
Architect: "Go ahead and make an intelligence check."
Player: "Uh oh, he's isn't exactly a genius..."
Architect: "You can try and roll anyway."
Player: "I rolled a 10, but his INT score is 7, so minus 3..."
Architect: "Yeah, that failed."
Killgrog: "It's a butterscotch recipe!"

When you make your character, consider taking points from areas where they are weak and placing theose points on abilities where they are strong. That way, when your character makes difficulty checks, they're more likely to succeed at actions they're good at and more likely to fail at actions they're bad at like in real life. Of course, the odd critical roll can always happen.

Critical Rolls

If you roll for a difficulty check or an attack and it lands on 1, the result is a critical miss. The action or attack you were trying to make fails instantly and often dramatically, and can even end up causing damage to your character depending on the situation. On the other hand, if you roll a natural 20, that is, a 20 without modifiers, it's known as a critical hit and it means that the action or attack is an instant success, sometimes even resulting in a one-hit-kill. The intensity of the action or attack is between you and the Architect to decide, the Player narrating the action and the Architect narrating the result of the action.

Advantage and Disadvantage

Some special moves, teamwork, and situations will grant the person rolling a dice something called advantage or disadvantage. When a Player has advantage on a roll, they roll twice and keep the higher roll as their result. In the case of disadvantage, the Player keeps the lower roll. Advantage and disadvantage can come from a character's special moves, environmental effects, curses, or just plain dumb luck courtesy of the Architect.

Combat

Eventually the heroes of the story will be met with obstacles that can only be moved by force. It's the easiest, most visceral way to add tension, excitement, and investment to the narrative. Combat is represented in-game by encounters, when the Players are encountering a challenge. It doesn't always have to be physical violence, but it must be a true test of the characters' abilities and the Player's problem solving skills.

Initiative

When an encounter begins, the Players will roll dexterity checks for each character they command and the Architect will do the same for each type of enemy involved in the encounter. The highest roll goes first, followed by second highest, etc. When the order is set, the character or creature with the highest roll get to take their turn by attacking or taking an action, whatever they want. If a character or creature has the element of surprise, they can attack before the initiative roll.

Attacking

Attacks can be done with unarmed strikes, weapons, spells, curses, summoned creatures, thrown objects... whatever the character or creature pleases. There are different conditions depending on the nature of the attack, however. First the attacker will make an attack roll using a d20, applying their appropriate ability score modifier.

Physical Strikes

If the attack is close to medium range and a physical strike with a sword, club, staff, pole, dagger, wrench, etc. then the attacker compares their attaack roll against the defender's armor class (AC). If the result is higher than their AC, then the attack hits and the attacker rolls their weapon's dice to see how much damage the defender will take. If the result is lower than their AC then the attack fails and play continues to the next character or creature in the order.

Saving Throws

Successful physical strikes cannot be defended against, but ranged attacks like arrows, spells, or bullets require the defender to roll what is called a saving throw, basically a dodge, using the appropriate ability score modifier. If their saving throw is higher than the attack roll, the defender successfully evades the attack. Saving throws can also be used to avoid environmental dangers and traps, set in place by the Architect.

Combat Table

The following are examples of different ability score modifiers an attacker would apply and saving throws a defender would roll, depending on the kind of attack being made:

Type Examples AS Mod Saving Throw
Physical Strike Punching, kicking, swinging a sword Str N/A
Ranged Throwing knives, shooting an arrow or bullets Str/Dex Dex
Physical Spell Fireballs, ice spikes, jets of scalding water Int/Wis Dex
Mental/Psychic Illusionary attacks, mind control Int/Wis Int/Wis
Poison/Disease Poison darts or gas, toxic sludge, plague rat bites Dex/Int Con

Health

All characters and creatures have another number representing their health, their life, called hit points. They represent the amount of damage your character can sustain before going unconcious and eventually dying. When an attack lands successfully the attacker will roll the damage die for the weapon or magic or whatever that was used. Weapons of different sizes have different damage dice, which is explained on the Character tab.

Characters can also revive hit points for themselves and/or others with special moves, first aid kits, health potions, or good old rest (outside of combat, of course).

Death

When a character's HP is reduced to zero the character is unconcious. The Player has 3 death saving throws as a way for their character to cling to life and give their party members a chance to heal them and bring them back from the brink. Healing may or may not wake them, but the character will no longer be dying. If the Player manages to roll 3 saves (3 rolls higher than 10) the character wakes with 1HP. If they fail all 3 saving throws before their party can stabilize them, they die.

Some Architects may allow for an afterlife of some kind as a way to keep the Player in the game or allow the Player to create a new character to bring in on the next quest, but it's recommended that ghost characters not be given corporeal power or a means to speak with the party unless a party member has a special move that allows them to do so. If death isn't a punishment then there's no risk in combat!

Architects: Make character death satisfying or temporary if you want to keep an amicable relationship with your Players.
